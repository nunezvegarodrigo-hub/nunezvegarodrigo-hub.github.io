Six months ago you bought the most complete CLM on the market. Hundreds of fields. Beautiful reports. A flawless demo. Today it is empty. And it is not the software's fault.

The problem is not the tool. It is the assumption that buying a tool is the same as building a system.

## Fewer tools, more system

I have seen legal departments with fifteen different tools. Contract review. Document management. E-signature. Intake. Reporting. Fifteen tools, five logins, three data silos, no integration. That is not a stack. That is a graveyard.

Less is more. One connected system beats fifteen disconnected ones. The goal is not to own software. It is to run legal like a business.

## The ideas that hold this up

- **Your CLM is empty because the process was never defined.** A tool enforces a standard. If there is no standard, there is nothing to enforce. Start with [[transformation|standardizing the process]].
- **Move from intake to intelligent triage.** Requests come from everywhere. Email, chat, hallway. Without a front door, everything is urgent and nothing is planned.
- **Legal tech is not an expense. It is a lifeline.** And it is a retention strategy. Your best lawyers will not stay to do repetitive work a system should handle.
- **Legal should not be the last to digitize.** Every other function measured itself years ago. Legal can catch up faster than it thinks.
- **No budget is not a blocker.** Start with analysis. The business case builds itself once you measure the work. See [[data-and-decisions|Data and Decision-Making]].

## Where this points

The reason tools sit empty is the same reason [[invisible-automation|Invisible Automation]] works. Stop asking people to feed the system. Build the system around how they already work.
